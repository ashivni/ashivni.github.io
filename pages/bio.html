Nacre

"Nacre" is the material that sea shells are made of. Anyone who has played with sea shells as a child knows that they are incredibly tough and strong. Why is this interesting? Afterall, nacre is not the toughest or the strongest material known to man (steel is way tougher, diamond is way stronger). While the absolute toughness of nacre is not interesting in itself, it is rather interesting when compared to the toughness of the materials that nacre is made up of. So, what exactly is nacre made up of? As it turns out, over 95% of nacre is black board chalk (aragonite). Now, chalk is neither hard nor tough. What of the remaining 5% or so? The remaining 5% is a sort of organic glue that holds the pieces of chalk together. And thats the mystery: How does nature take two rather ordinary ingredients, namely chalk and some glue, and make something incredibly strong and tough like sea shells? This mystery, or some version of it, exists in all the nature's structural materials, including wood, tooth, bones, turtle shells, antlers, and so on.

The high magnification SEM (Scanning Electorn Microscope) shown above (Fig. 1) sheds some light on the actitecture of nacre. As the figure shows, nacre is made out of thin bricks of aragonite arranged in a manner similar to the bricks in a wall. This is the so-called brick and mortar structure. The role of the mortar is played by the organic glue. Figure 2 shows an idealized 2D cross-section of this structure. Note that this figure is not accurate in scale. In reality the mortar (organic glue) layer is much thinner, and the bricks have a much higher aspect ratio. As one can imagine, this structure can make crack propagation tougher because the strain can get localized in the ductile mortar phase, and because cracks can get deflected at any of the multiple interfaces.

Unit Cell Analysis

A very simple analysis can be used to obtain the elastic moduli of materials with a brick and mortar structure. In this analysis we make a number of simplifying assumptions. We assume that the thickness of the mortar is small compared to the thickness of the bricks, which in turn is small compared to the width of the bricks, i.e, $$ t_1, t_2 \ll h \ll w.$$ The validity of these assumptions is evident from the observed geometry of nacre. Further, we assume that the bricks can be treated as rigid, i.e., all the strain is localized in the mortar phase. This assumption is also well justified since the bricks are much stronger than the mortar phase. We will consider the implications and limitations of this assumption in detail later. For the purpose of this analysis we assume that the unit cell has a vertical axis of symmetry, i.e., \(e = 0\) in figure 3. The resulting unit cell is shown in figure 4.

Let us first see why a general 2D material is characterized by 6 elastic contants. There are several ways of thinking about this. I prefer to think as follows. The elastic constants give mapping between the stress and the strain. The matrix representing this linear map must be symmetric since it is obtained as a second gradient of the energy density function. The most general such mapping can be written as $$ \begin{bmatrix}\sigma_{11} \\ \sigma_{22} \\ \sigma_{12} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} & c_{12} & c_{13} \\ c_{12} & c_{22} & c_{23} \\ c_{13} & c_{23} & c_{33} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\epsilon_{11} \\ \epsilon_{22} \\ 2\epsilon_{12} \end{bmatrix}, $$ involving a total of 6 independent elastic constants. Now, due to the vertical symmetry axis, a strain in the 11-direction cannot lead to a stress response in the 12-direction. This gives \(c_{13} = 0\). Similarly, due to the horizontal symmetry axis we get \(c_{23} = 0\). We further assume that the mortar material has zero poison's ratio. This combined with the assumption of rigid bricks leads to \(c_{12} = 0\). This massacre of elastic constants leaves us with 3 unknown constants, and the stress-strain relation reads $$ \begin{bmatrix}\sigma_{11} \\ \sigma_{22} \\ \sigma_{12} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & c_{22} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & c_{33} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\epsilon_{11} \\ \epsilon_{22} \\ 2\epsilon_{12} \end{bmatrix}. $$ Of all our assumptions, the one leading to \( c_{12} = 0 \) is perhaps the weakest, while the vertical symmetry axis is the second weakest. We stick with these for the time being in favor of simplicity. Our next task is to find the 3 remaining elastic constants. For this purpose we further assume \( t_1 = t_2 = t \).

The analysis here is rather simple and covers only the elastic regime, however, the assumption of rigid bricks simplifies the problem a lot, and there is hope that under this assumption one can solve the more general elastic problem. Let us first apply a uniaxial strain \(\epsilon_{11}\). The net deformation of the unit cell in the 1-direction is then \(\delta = (w + t)\epsilon_{11}\). Then, the elastic energy of the unit cell (per unit depth) can be written as \begin{equation} \psi(\delta) = 2 h t \left( \frac{1}{2} E \left(\frac{\delta}{t}\right)^2 \right) + 4 \frac{w}{2}t \left(\frac{1}{2}G\left(\frac{\delta}{2t}\right)^2 \right), \end{equation} where \(E, G\) are the Young's modulus and the shear modulus of the mortar, respectively. The effective modulus of the unit cell in the 1 direction is \( c_{11}\), thus the corresponding effective energy would be \begin{equation} \psi(\delta) = 2(h+t)(w+t) \left(\frac{1}{2}c_{11}\left(\frac{\delta}{w+t}\right)^2 \right). \end{equation} Equating the above two expressions, we get \begin{equation} c_{11} = E \frac{h}{t}\frac{w+t}{h+t} \left( 1 + \frac{wG}{4hE}\right) \approx E \frac{w}{t}\left( 1 + \frac{wG}{4hE}\right) \approx G\frac{w^2}{4ht}. \end{equation} Similarly, if we apply a uniaxial strain \( \epsilon_{22} \), then the deformation in the 2-direction is \( \delta = 2(h+t)\epsilon_{22} \), and the net elastic energy of the unit cell (per unit depth) is approximately \begin{equation} \psi(\delta) = 4\frac{wt}{2}\left( \frac{1}{2}E\left(\frac{\delta}{2t}\right)^2\right), \end{equation} while the effective energy for the cell is (approximately) \begin{equation} \psi(\delta) = 2hw \left( \frac{1}{2}c_{22}\left(\frac{\delta}{2h}\right)^2\right). \end{equation} Equating the above two expressions we get \begin{equation} c_{22} \approx E \frac{h}{t}. \end{equation} Finally, by using similar arguments for a simple \( \epsilon_{12} \) deformation, we get \begin{equation} c_{12} \approx G\frac{h}{2t} \end{equation}

How good are the assumptions that we made in the above derivations? The geometrical assumptions \( t \ll h \ll w\) are fairy well justified. What about the assumption of rigid bricks? That assumption is well justified so long as the strain is mostly localized in the mortar. Another way of thinking about this is that if nacre is much less stiffer than aragonite then our approximations are good. It turns out that while nacre is less stiffer than aragonite, its not much less stiffer. Thus our approximations are questionable. The model can thus be improved upon by including more realistic deformation. In fact, Begley et. al. have done such an analysis for a uniaxial deformation. This also shows that nature does not work to make our analysis simpler!
